Project 01 · Individual assignment

Common Tools for Health Informatics (G) + Clinical Informatics (CI)

A patient-centred conceptual information model for structuring Type 2 diabetes care data in a clear, consistent, and reusable way.

Skills demonstrated

Objective

Develop a patient-centred UML information model for Type 2 diabetes care that demonstrates how structured clinical data can be represented clearly and consistently using recognised clinical terminologies.

Project overview

The model explores how clinical information can support care, documentation, and later analysis without losing patient-level detail.

The patient is the central class. Glucose measurements, HbA1c results, conditions, exercise treatment, medication prescriptions, and clinical encounters connect directly to that patient so changes in care can be followed over time.

Clinical vocabularies

The assignment applies established vocabularies to keep clinical data structured and interpretable. ICD-10 represents diagnoses, FASS describes medication products and attributes, and ATC supports the classification and grouping of medicines.

  • ICD-10 for conditions and diagnostic classification
  • FASS for substance, strength, and pharmaceutical form
  • ATC for therapeutic and pharmacological grouping
  • Defined values for exercise type and intensity

Design decisions

Person is modelled as a superclass shared by patients and healthcare professionals, avoiding duplicated personal attributes. Encounters act as the link between a patient and the professionals involved in care. Medication prescriptions are separated from medication concepts so the treatment instruction remains distinct from the drug description.

Key reflections

Translating a practical clinical workflow into a structured system model requires a different way of thinking.

My clinical background helped me identify the important care elements, while the modelling work showed how relationships and intermediaries can make complex information easier to maintain and use digitally.

Building an openEHR Clinical Composition

Objective

Transform a clinical encounter scenario into a structured openEHR composition using reusable clinical archetypes and a logically organised information model.

Select a section to see its archetypes and clinical examples.

Project overview

The central part of the examination was building an openEHR composition for a complete clinical encounter.

I used COMPOSITION.encounter.v1 as the container and organised the clinical information into five logical sections. Each section contains selected openEHR archetypes that represent the information needed during the consultation.

Selected archetypes

The composition combines evaluation, observation, cluster, instruction, and action archetypes so the record follows the progression from the patient’s reason for seeking care to the clinician’s management plan.

  • Reason for encounter, gender, and age assertion
  • Story/history and symptom/sign documentation
  • Clinical synopsis and health-risk assessment
  • Medication orders, medication actions, and service requests

Why this structure matters

Organising the composition as Background, Clinical History, Clinical Assessment, Past Medications, and Management Plan mirrors the workflow of a consultation. The modular archetypes make individual data elements easier to interpret and reuse in follow-up visits, referrals, and treatment planning.

Methods and supporting work

Information-flow mapping, UML modelling, ICD-10, and ATC supported the composition by clarifying where information originates, how clinical concepts relate, and how diagnoses and medicines can be represented consistently. The openEHR composition remained the main deliverable.

Critical evaluation

Standardisation improves consistency, but the model still needs the right clinical context and collaboration to be safe and useful.

I evaluated both advantages and limitations, including structured-data reuse, clearer documentation, incomplete integration of identity and demographic information, cognitive burden from separated components, and the risk of oversimplifying complex clinical situations.

Designing and Evaluating a Maternal Health CDSS

Objective

Design a user-centred clinical decision support system (CDSS) concept that supports maternal healthcare professionals in geographically isolated settings by improving structured assessment, risk identification, and referral planning.

System concept

Supporting decisions from first contact to referral

The proposed workflow combines information from the patient with a structured professional assessment and clear referral support.

  1. 01 Patient Pre-consultation form

    Pregnancy and medical history are submitted before the visit.

  2. 02 Healthcare professional Maternal assessment

    A structured checklist guides collection of clinical information.

  3. 03 Decision support Risk guidance

    The CDSS highlights risk factors and suggests appropriate next steps.

  4. 04 Care coordination Electronic referral

    High-risk cases can be communicated to the receiving hospital.

Project details

Project overview

The expandable sections summarise the analysis behind the CDSS concept, including user needs, system requirements, evaluation planning, and implementation considerations.

01 Clinical context Clinical setting and users Who the system supports and why it is needed
Setting Isolated maternity-care centres

Referral hospitals may be two hours away.

Primary users Nurses and midwives

Professionals work without a physician on site.

Patient user Pregnant woman seeking care

Provides history before the clinical assessment.

Referral-hospital professionals and health authorities are additional stakeholders.

02 System requirements What the system must support Three clear and testable requirements
  1. 01

    Display a structured maternal and perinatal assessment checklist.

  2. 02

    Let the patient submit relevant history before the visit.

  3. 03

    Automatically save interrupted assessments so work can resume safely.

03 Evaluation strategy How the concept would be assessed Clinical outcomes, timing and validation
Outcome 01 Complication detection

Time to detection and correctly identified high-risk cases.

Outcome 02 Referral delays

Time from risk detection to referral submission.

Validation Clinical appropriateness

Review of referrals by receiving clinicians.

04 Future considerations What would be needed next User testing and clinical validation

Personas support focus, but can hide differences in skills, workload, and local resources.

The concept would require direct user involvement, usability testing, and clinical validation before use in practice.

Key reflections

Designing a clinical decision support concept highlighted how successful digital health systems depend as much on understanding users and clinical workflows as on technology itself. Translating clinical practice into structured requirements reinforced the importance of user-centred design, patient safety, and multidisciplinary collaboration throughout system development.

Project 04 · Four-person group research project

Interpreting Laboratory Test Results in 1177

A survey-based evaluation of how Swedish-speaking adults without medical backgrounds interpret laboratory results presented in a simulated 1177 Journalen interface.

Course

Methods
Scenario-based survey, descriptive statistics, Spearman correlation and descriptive content analysis
Focus
Consumer health informatics and patient understanding
Tools
  • Google Forms
  • IBM SPSS
  • Sw-eHEALS
Skills demonstrated
  • Research design
  • Survey methodology
  • Statistical analysis
  • Qualitative content analysis
  • Human-centred evaluation

Objective

Examine how adults without medical backgrounds interpret laboratory test results presented in a simulated 1177 Journalen interface, assess whether self-reported eHealth literacy is associated with interpretation performance, and identify common barriers to understanding.

Study process

From patient portal to research findings

  1. 01 Simulated 1177 interface
  2. 02 105 participants
  3. 03 Scenario-based survey + Sw-eHEALS
  4. 04 Statistical + qualitative analysis
  5. 05 Findings on user understanding

Project overview

The study investigated whether laboratory values, reference ranges, and abnormality indicators were sufficient for non-medical users to understand their results. Using simulated data rather than real patient records, it examined interpretation accuracy, self-reported eHealth literacy, and user-reported difficulties and missing information.

Research design

Participants

111 people completed the questionnaire, and 105 were included in the final analysis.

Scenario-based survey

Participants interpreted four laboratory-result scenarios presented in a simulated 1177-style interface.

Quantitative analysis

Interpretation accuracy, perceived concern, and Sw-eHEALS scores were analysed using descriptive statistics and Spearman correlation.

Qualitative analysis

Open-ended responses were analysed using descriptive content analysis to identify recurring barriers.

Key findings

Interpretation varied

Accuracy was higher for clearly normal or clearly abnormal results than for results near reference boundaries or only mildly abnormal.

eHealth literacy was not significantly associated

Self-reported Sw-eHEALS scores were not significantly associated with interpretation accuracy or perceived concern.

Users needed more context

Participants reported difficulty understanding terminology, reference ranges, and the clinical meaning of results.

Next steps were unclear

Participants wanted clearer guidance on whether they should wait, contact healthcare, or seek further information.

My contribution

This was a four-person group research project. I led the analysis and discussion of the qualitative research question concerning user-reported barriers and difficulties. I also contributed to the study design and manuscript development and was responsible for final editing and reference management.

Key reflections

This project reinforced that access to digital health information does not automatically lead to understanding. Combining interpretation tasks with users’ own descriptions of difficulty showed the value of evaluating both measurable performance and lived experience when studying patient-facing systems. The project also highlighted the importance of clear explanations, contextual support, and guidance on appropriate next steps.
